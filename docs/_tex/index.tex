\subsection{Computational modeling of the Early-LPP contribution to
emotional
recall}\label{computational-modeling-of-the-early-lpp-contribution-to-emotional-recall}

The behavioral and EEG analyses identified two forms of variation in
recall. At the category level, negative items were recalled more often
than neutral items. At the item level, Early LPP was more strongly
associated with subsequent recall for negative than neutral items. The
empirical analyses distinguish these patterns statistically, but they do
not determine how trial-level Early LPP should be mapped onto the
encoding mechanism proposed to produce emotional memory enhancement. We
therefore tested whether using item-level Early LPP to scale learning
strength could replace a category-wide increase in learning strength, or
whether the two forms of modulation explained complementary variation in
which items were recalled.

We organized this comparison around three focused questions. We tested
(1) whether preferential learning of negative items improved prediction
of which items were recalled; (2) whether scaling learning with
item-level Early LPP could replace that category-wide advantage or
improve prediction after the advantage was included; and (3) whether
Early-LPP scaling was better applied to both negative and neutral items
or only to negative items. We therefore crossed the presence or absence
of preferential emotional learning with three LPP specifications: no
LPP-based learning modulation, one relationship shared across negative
and neutral items, or one relationship limited to negative items. This
crossing produced six models. All six models used the same memory
architecture. We first describe that shared architecture and show where
preferential emotional learning and Early LPP altered learning strength.
We then describe model fitting, compare the six models, and use
simulations to determine which observed patterns the fitted models
reproduced.

\subsubsection{eCMR Model Specification}\label{ecmr-model-specification}

We modeled recall with an adaptation of emotional Context Maintenance
and Retrieval (eCMR) (\citeproc{ref-talmi2019retrieved}{Talmi et al.,
2019}). Like other retrieved-context models
(\citeproc{ref-howard2002distributed}{Howard \& Kahana, 2002};
\citeproc{ref-polyn2009context}{Polyn et al., 2009}), eCMR represents
context as a continuously updated internal state that carries
information from recently processed events. Studying an item changes
this state and forms associations in both directions: the item can later
reinstate aspects of its study context, and the current context can
later provide retrieval support for the item. During free recall, items
receiving greater support are more likely to be selected. Recalling an
item then reinstates aspects of its study context, changing the cue that
guides the next recall. In the present comparison, stronger
context-to-item learning could therefore increase an item's probability
of entering the recalled set. We first specify this common retrieval
mechanism and then define the learning-strength terms that differed
across models.

Published eCMR distinguishes temporal and source information within the
context representation. Temporal context represents the evolving
sequence of recently processed events, whereas source context represents
non-temporal information about an event---in the present model, whether
an item was negative or neutral. Recalling an item reinstates both
components, so its category-related source state can increase support
for other items associated with similar source information. Published
eCMR also allows preferential learning to strengthen the associations
formed by negative items (\citeproc{ref-talmi2019retrieved}{Talmi et
al., 2019}). All six models retained this source-representation
mechanism: every model represented negative and neutral source
information. The comparison varied the learning-strength terms applied
within that shared representation, so a model without preferential
emotional learning still distinguished negative and neutral items in
source context.

The fitted model was an adaptation rather than an unchanged
implementation of published eCMR. It omitted item-specific semantic
similarities among the pictures and the published source-switch
mechanism, in which a transition between negative and neutral categories
produces additional disruption of temporal context. We omitted that
mechanism because recall order was unavailable to diagnose
category-boundary effects and because the present comparison concerned
learning strength rather than temporal disruption at source transitions.

The implementation also differed from published eCMR in how it combined
old and new context and in how it converted retrieval support into
recall choices. We identify those differences when the corresponding
operations are introduced below. These choices were fixed across all six
model definitions, so their structural differences concerned the
learning-strength terms rather than different retrieval architectures.

Within each list \(j\), each studied item \(i\) occupied a unique
one-hot feature slot \(\mathbf f_i\): one entry was 1 and all remaining
entries were zero. The complete context state therefore contained the
temporal and source components introduced above,

\[
\mathbf c_i=
\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf c_i^T\\
\mathbf c_i^S
\end{bmatrix}.
\]

The two components were updated and normalized separately, but together
formed one context representation rather than independent memory
systems. Both components included a start-of-list state, and all context
states and associations were initialized anew for each list.

Items and context interacted through two associative mappings with
opposite directions. With column-vector notation, the complete
feature-to-context mapping \(M^{FC}\) transformed an item into
contextual input, whereas the complete context-to-feature mapping
\(M^{CF}\) transformed the current context into support for candidate
items:

\[
M^{FC}=
\begin{bmatrix}
M_T^{FC}\\
M_S^{FC}
\end{bmatrix},
\qquad
M^{CF}=
\begin{bmatrix}
M_T^{CF} & M_S^{CF}
\end{bmatrix}.
\]

Core symbols are collected in Supplementary
Table~\ref{tbl-model-notation}.

Before study, the two associative mappings were initialized differently.
The temporal block of the initial feature-to-context mapping associated
each item with its own temporal feature, and the source block associated
each item with its negative or neutral source feature; both had weight
\(1-\gamma\). In the initial temporal context-to-feature block, the
context feature paired with an item supplied support \(\delta\), whereas
every other item-associated context feature supplied background support
\(\alpha\). The source context-to-feature block began at zero, so source
context supported items only through associations learned during study.

When an item was presented, each context component first received
unit-length input from the corresponding feature-to-context block:

\[
\mathbf u_i^T=\mathcal N\!\left(M_T^{FC}\mathbf f_i\right),
\qquad
\mathbf u_i^S=\mathcal N\!\left(M_S^{FC}\mathbf f_i\right),
\]

where \(\mathcal N(\mathbf v)=\mathbf v/\sqrt{\sum_k v_k^2+\epsilon}\)
and \(\epsilon\) is machine epsilon. Each preceding context state then
moved toward its input. Smaller encoding-drift values retained more of
the preceding state; larger values gave the current item greater
influence.

The fitted implementation performed this movement with the following
componentwise integration operator:

\[
\begin{aligned}
\boldsymbol\rho(\mathbf c,\mathbf u,\beta)
&=
\sqrt{
\mathbf 1+\beta^2
\left[
(\mathbf c\odot\mathbf u)^2-\mathbf 1
\right]
}
-
\beta(\mathbf c\odot\mathbf u),\\
\mathcal U(\mathbf c,\mathbf u,\beta)
&=
\mathcal N\!\left[
\boldsymbol\rho(\mathbf c,\mathbf u,\beta)\odot\mathbf c
+
\beta\mathbf u
\right].
\end{aligned}
\]

Here, \(\odot\), the square, and the square root operate elementwise.
The retention vector \(\boldsymbol\rho\) controlled how much of each
dimension of the preceding state remained after the new input was
incorporated. Temporal and source context were then updated separately:

\[
\mathbf c_i^T
=
\mathcal U(\mathbf c_{i-1}^T,\mathbf u_i^T,\beta_{\mathrm{enc}}^T),
\qquad
\mathbf c_i^S
=
\mathcal U(\mathbf c_{i-1}^S,\mathbf u_i^S,\beta_{\mathrm{enc}}^S).
\]

This componentwise retention-and-renormalization rule differs from the
scalar retention coefficient in the published CMR and eCMR equations.

Both context components were updated before the item's associations were
learned. The item was therefore bound to temporal and source states that
it had already helped create, rather than to the states that preceded
its presentation. This post-update order was held fixed across the
comparison.

Feature-to-context learning stored the updated state that a later
presentation or recall of the item could reinstate. The same fitted rate
\(\gamma\) applied to the temporal and source blocks:

\[
\Delta M_{T,i}^{FC}
=
\gamma\mathbf c_i^T\mathbf f_i^{\mathsf T},
\qquad
\Delta M_{S,i}^{FC}
=
\gamma\mathbf c_i^S\mathbf f_i^{\mathsf T}.
\]

Context-to-feature learning stored the reverse associations through
which context could later support the item:

\[
\Delta M_{T,i}^{CF}
=
\phi_i\mathbf f_i(\mathbf c_i^T)^{\mathsf T},
\qquad
\Delta M_{S,i}^{CF}
=
S_{ij}^S\mathbf f_i(\mathbf c_i^S)^{\mathsf T}.
\]

The temporal learning strength \(\phi_i=1+\phi_s\exp[-\phi_d(i-1)]\)
gave early list positions a temporary advantage: \(\phi_s\) controlled
its size and \(\phi_d\) controlled its decay. The source learning
strength \(S_{ij}^S\) contained the same primacy gradient together with
the emotional-learning and Early-LPP terms defined below. Placing both
focal predictors on source context-to-item learning gave them one
directly comparable route to recall, while temporal context-to-item
learning remained unchanged.

At recall onset, \(\beta_{\mathrm{start}}\) shifted both context
components toward their start-of-list states. The current temporal and
source states then supplied additive support for each item that had not
yet been recalled:

\[
a_i
=
\left[M^{CF}\mathbf c\right]_i
=
\left[M_T^{CF}\mathbf c^T\right]_i
+
\left[M_S^{CF}\mathbf c^S\right]_i.
\]

The choice-sensitivity parameter \(\tau_c\) converted support into
choice weights proportional to \(a_i^{\tau_c}\). The implementation
evaluated these powers in log space and rescaled all weights by a common
constant to avoid numerical overflow; this rescaling preserved their
relative values. After machine epsilon was added to each available
item's weight, let \(q_i\) denote the resulting positive value. The
probability of the next recalled item was

\[
P(R_t=i\mid\mathcal A_t)
=
\frac{q_i}{\sum_{k\in\mathcal A_t}q_k},
\qquad i\in\mathcal A_t,
\]

where \(\mathcal A_t\) is the set of studied items still available at
recall step \(t\). Larger \(\tau_c\) made the model more likely to
select the item with greater support.

After an item was recalled, temporal context moved toward its retrieved
temporal input at rate \(\beta_{\mathrm{rec}}^T\), and source context
fully moved toward its retrieved source input by fixing
\(\beta_{\mathrm{rec}}^S=1\). The retrieved source input contained both
the item's category feature and the source state learned during study.

Full source reinstatement was a fixed modeling constraint, not a
conclusion from the data: without recall order, we treated partial
source reinstatement as difficult to distinguish from source-learning
strength and recall-choice sensitivity. The recalled item was then
removed from the available set, so repeated recalls were disallowed.
Context-guided selection and reinstatement complete the pathway through
which source learning strength can change which items enter the recalled
set.

\subsubsection{Encoding-strength
hypotheses}\label{encoding-strength-hypotheses}

Every model estimated a shared source-learning baseline
\(\omega\phi_i\). The parameter \(\omega\) scaled source learning
relative to temporal learning, for which the corresponding scale was
fixed at 1. Estimating \(\omega\) in every model allowed source context
to contribute even when neither focal mechanism was enabled. The first
focal term allowed negative items to receive a category-wide
emotional-learning multiplier on this shared baseline.

Preferential emotional learning was represented by
\(\phi_{\mathrm{emot}}^{e_i}\), where \(e_i=1\) for a negative item and
\(e_i=0\) for a neutral item. The multiplier therefore equaled
\(\phi_{\mathrm{emot}}\) for negative items and 1 for neutral items.
Models without the preferential emotional-learning term fixed
\(\phi_{\mathrm{emot}}=1\), whereas models testing preferential
emotional learning estimated the multiplier over the range \((0,10]\).
Values above 1 strengthened source context-to-item learning for negative
items relative to neutral items, whereas values below 1 attenuated it.
That range allowed attenuation as well as enhancement of negative-item
learning; ``preferential emotional learning'' names the directional
hypothesis, and interpreting the fitted term as enhancement requires an
estimate above 1. The category-wide emotional-learning multiplier can
change the average recall difference between negative and neutral items,
but it cannot distinguish two negative items on the basis of their
different Early-LPP amplitudes. The Early-LPP terms supplied that
item-level variation.

For item \(i\) on 20-item study-test list \(j\), the model used
within-list mean-centered Early LPP,

\[
z_{ij}
=
\mathrm{EarlyLPP}_{ij}
-
\overline{\mathrm{EarlyLPP}}_j.
\]

A positive value of \(z_{ij}\) means that an item elicited a larger
Early LPP than the average item on the same list; a negative value means
that it elicited a smaller one. This predictor isolates within-list
variation and removes differences between list means by construction.
The present models can therefore test whether relatively larger Early
LPP predicts which items enter a list's recalled set, but not whether
lists with larger average Early LPP produce more recalls. The preceding
empirical regression did not separate within-list variation from
differences among list means and participants. The computational
coefficient addresses only the within-list relationship and is therefore
not a numerical counterpart of the empirical regression coefficient.

Source learning strength was the product of the ordinary source-learning
baseline, the category-wide emotional-learning multiplier, and an
item-level Early-LPP multiplier:

\[
S_{ij}^S
=
\omega\phi_i
\times
\phi_{\mathrm{emot}}^{e_i}
\times
\exp\!\left[
\kappa z_{ij}
+
\kappa_{\mathrm{emot}}e_i z_{ij}
\right].
\]

The coefficient \(\kappa\) is the General Early-LPP slope on log
learning strength, and \(\kappa_{\mathrm{emot}}\) is an additional
negative-item slope. The resulting log-learning slope is \(\kappa\) for
neutral items and \(\kappa+\kappa_{\mathrm{emot}}\) for negative items.
At the list mean, where \(z_{ij}=0\), the Early-LPP multiplier equals 1
and the model recovers its ordinary category-level learning baseline.
The exponential link guarantees positive learning strength, but
positivity is not its only implication: the link assumes that a one-unit
Early-LPP difference produces a proportional rather than a constant
absolute change in learning. The model comparison tests whether one such
proportional relationship improves prediction when it applies to both
categories or only to negative items.

No-LPP models fixed both Early-LPP coefficients at zero. General-LPP
models estimated \(\kappa\) and fixed \(\kappa_{\mathrm{emot}}=0\),
applying one slope to negative and neutral items. Emotion-dependent-LPP
models fixed \(\kappa=0\) and estimated \(\kappa_{\mathrm{emot}}\),
applying one slope only to negative items. General and Emotion-dependent
models therefore estimated the same number of parameters and differed
only in the items to which their single Early-LPP slope applied. The
empirical interaction motivated this matched comparison, but the model
comparison determined which mapping better predicted the recalled sets.

The primary grid does not estimate General and emotion-dependent
Early-LPP coefficients simultaneously. It therefore asks whether one
coefficient is more useful when shared by both categories or limited to
negative items; it does not test whether a residual General component is
exactly zero after an emotion-dependent component is fitted. The same
learning-strength equation also defines a Full-LPP mapping in which
\(\kappa\) and \(\kappa_{\mathrm{emot}}\) are estimated together. In
that mapping, \(\kappa_{\mathrm{emot}}\) is formally an additional
negative-item slope; when \(\kappa=0\), it supplies the entire
negative-item slope. We fitted the two source-context Full-LPP variants
exploratorily, but their best-found solutions did not consistently
recover the likelihoods of their nested one-slope models. We therefore
excluded them from the primary comparison and report them separately as
optimization-incomplete fits.

The comparison also holds source context constant across all six models.
It can test learning-strength terms within that architecture, but it
cannot establish that the predictive Early-LPP relationship is specific
to source-context rather than temporal-context learning. Having defined
the alternatives, we next describe the common evidence used to compare
them.

\subsubsection{Pooled estimation and
evaluation}\label{pooled-estimation-and-evaluation}

The modeling dataset matched each of the 6,840 study events to its
recall outcome and Early-LPP value. As described in the preceding EEG
methods, each single-trial ERP was first standardized relative to its
own prestimulus interval, using the mean and standard deviation from
\(-200\) to 0 ms separately for each electrode. Early LPP was then
extracted from the prespecified 400--1000 ms window and centroparietal
electrodes.

Early-LPP values were unavailable for 371 events (5.4\%). Before
within-list centering, these values were imputed in three steps: first
from available items of the same condition on the same participant's
list, then from all available items on that list if the
condition-specific mean was unavailable, and finally from the
dataset-wide mean for the corresponding condition. Recall status was not
used in the imputation. This procedure yielded one complete 20-item
input sequence for each list.

Each model was fitted with one parameter vector shared across all 342
study-test lists from 38 participants. The pooled analysis asks whether
one parameterization captures the population-level pattern across the
full dataset. It does not estimate participant-specific model parameters
or individual differences. This restriction is important because each
participant contributed only nine lists, too few independent list
histories to support stable estimation of 11 to 13 parameters per
participant.

The available recall records identified which items were recalled, but
their output order was not available for modeling. This creates a
problem because eCMR assigns probability to an ordered recall sequence:
after each recall, the retrieved item changes context and therefore
changes the probabilities of subsequent recalls. An unordered set of
\(n_j\) items is compatible with \(n_j!\) possible orders. Enumerating
all of those orders was impractical, so we approximated the evidence for
each recalled set using 50 sampled orders.

Let \(\mathcal S_j\) denote the items recalled from list \(j\), let
\(n_j=|\mathcal S_j|\), let \(\pi_{jk}\) be the \(k\)th sampled ordering
of those items, and let \(\boldsymbol\theta\) denote a model's parameter
vector. For each list, the model was supplied with \(n_j\) and evaluated
on which studied items composed \(\mathcal S_j\). Using \(K=50\) fixed
sampled orders, the fitted score for the set was

\[
\widetilde P(\mathcal S_j\mid\boldsymbol\theta,n_j)
=
\frac{1}{K}
\sum_{k=1}^{K}
\prod_{t=1}^{n_j}
P\!\left(
R_{jt}=\pi_{jk,t}
\mid
\pi_{jk,1:(t-1)},
\boldsymbol\theta
\right).
\]

Each product in this expression is the probability of one possible
ordered recall sequence; the outer average combines the 50 sampled
sequences. Multiplying the average by \(n_j!\) would estimate the full
sum over compatible orders. Because this factor depends only on the
observed list and not on the model parameters, omitting it adds the same
constant to every negative log-likelihood (NLL). It therefore does not
change fitted parameters; after model complexity is considered, it
likewise does not change differences or rankings under Akaike's
information criterion (AIC), defined below. The same sampled orders and
random seed were used for every model, so differences between models
could not arise from evaluating different order samples.

Termination events were excluded from the likelihood. Accordingly, the
model predicts which items enter the recalled set conditional on the
number of items the participant recalled from that list. The analysis
does not explain why that number of items was recalled and does not test
a process for deciding when recall stops. Stopping may depend on the
state of retrieval in free recall, but evaluating such a process
requires a separate, validated termination model. The current likelihood
keeps the present comparison focused on recalled-item identity.

We minimized the summed negative log of the approximate set scores using
differential evolution, a population-based parameter search
(\citeproc{ref-storn1997differential}{Storn \& Price, 1997}). Each
optimization began with a Latin-hypercube sample that spread its initial
candidate vectors across the allowed parameter space. The search
population contained 15 candidate vectors per free parameter, yielding
165, 180, or 195 candidates for models with 11, 12, or 13 free
parameters, respectively. The crossover probability was 0.9, the
differential weight varied between 0.5 and 1.0, and the relative
convergence tolerance was \(10^{-4}\). Optimization continued for at
most 1,000 generations and was repeated from three independent starts;
the lowest NLL found across the three starts was retained.

The Early-LPP coefficients were constrained to be nonnegative in
accordance with the directional empirical hypothesis. Their upper bound
of 0.2145 limited either Early-LPP term to a tenfold multiplier at the
largest observed centered Early LPP. The resulting estimates and
rankings are conditional on this search budget and these parameter
ranges.

Lower NLL indicates that a model assigned greater probability to the
observed recalled sets. We compared the best-found solutions using
Akaike's information criterion, \(\mathrm{AIC}=2\mathrm{NLL}+2p\), where
\(p\) is the number of free parameters
(\citeproc{ref-burnham2002model}{Burnham \& Anderson, 2002}). Lower AIC
indicates a better trade-off between conditional fit and model
complexity. AIC establishes whether an optional learning term improved
prediction enough to offset its additional parameter, but AIC does not
show which empirical pattern that term reproduced. We therefore paired
the model comparisons with simulations designed to make the fitted terms
interpretable.

For each fitted parameter vector, we generated 200 complete simulated
datasets containing the same 342 study lists and the same item-level
Early-LPP values as the observed dataset. For each list, we generated
exactly its observed number of recalled items, matching the conditioning
used in the likelihood.

We summarized two patterns in each simulated dataset: the
negative-versus-neutral recall difference and the
remembered-versus-forgotten difference in within-list centered Early
LPP. The parameters changed which studied items the model recalled; they
did not change the observed Early-LPP value attached to an item. These
summaries were not optimized as separate fitting targets. They are
therefore explanatory diagnostics of the fitted recalled-set model, not
independent or out-of-sample validation.

\subsubsection{Contribution of preferential emotional
learning}\label{contribution-of-preferential-emotional-learning}

The first comparison asks whether preferential learning of negative
items improves prediction of which items are recalled. Freeing the
emotional-learning multiplier lowered AIC under every Early-LPP
specification: by 113.639 points with no LPP, by 97.238 points with
General LPP, and by 78.768 points with Emotion-dependent LPP. The fitted
multiplier exceeded 1 in all three models, so the estimated term
enhanced rather than attenuated negative-item learning. The recall-rate
panels explain this consistent advantage. Models with preferential
emotional learning produced negative-minus-neutral recall differences
between 0.118 and 0.134, close to the observed difference of 0.124;
models without preferential emotional learning produced differences
between 0.001 and 0.019. Because every model represented negative and
neutral source features, this result concerns preferential emotional
learning rather than the mere presence of category information.
Preferential emotional learning supplied category-level recall
structure, leaving the item-level contribution of Early LPP as the next
question.

\subsubsection{Contribution of Early
LPP}\label{contribution-of-early-lpp}

The second comparison asks whether Early LPP adds predictive information
beyond a model with no EEG input. Without preferential emotional
learning, General LPP lowered AIC by 23.582 points and Emotion-dependent
LPP lowered it by 63.676 points relative to the source-context baseline.
With preferential emotional learning, the corresponding AIC improvements
were 7.182 and 28.805 points. Early LPP therefore improved conditional
prediction when the emotional-learning multiplier was fixed and when it
was estimated.

The equal-complexity substitution comparisons asked a different question
and produced a different result. The Preferential-learning model had an
AIC 90.057 points lower than the General-LPP model and 49.963 points
lower than the Emotion-dependent-LPP model; all three models had 12 free
parameters. Within the fitted specification and parameter ranges,
neither LPP-derived scaling rule matched the free emotional-learning
multiplier, even though Early LPP improved prediction when added to
models with or without that multiplier. This pattern favors
complementarity over substitution.

The Early-LPP panels identify the additional pattern captured by
Early-LPP modulation. In the observed data, remembered items had a 0.403
larger mean centered Early LPP than forgotten items within the negative
category, whereas the corresponding neutral-item difference was 0.021.
Models without Early-LPP modulation produced little
remembered-versus-forgotten separation in either category. Because the
Early-LPP values themselves remained fixed, remembered--forgotten
differences arose only when a model preferentially recalled items with
larger values. Early-LPP modulation therefore supplied item-level
information that the category-wide emotional-learning multiplier did not
contain. Because both Early-LPP mappings added information, the final
comparison asks where their single slope should apply.

\subsubsection{General versus Emotion-dependent Early
LPP}\label{general-versus-emotion-dependent-early-lpp}

The third comparison contrasts one slope shared across categories with
one slope limited to negative items. The two alternatives had equal
numbers of parameters, so their AIC difference reflected fit rather than
a difference in the complexity penalty. Emotion-dependent LPP lowered
AIC by 40.094 points relative to General LPP when preferential emotional
learning was absent and by 21.623 points when preferential emotional
learning was present. The simulations clarify why: General LPP separated
remembered and forgotten items in both categories, whereas
Emotion-dependent LPP concentrated the separation among negative items,
as in the observed pattern. When the models were each given one
Early-LPP coefficient, that coefficient was therefore more useful when
applied to negative items.

This comparison is conditional on the declared parameter ranges. In the
model combining preferential emotional learning with General LPP,
\(\hat\kappa=0.208\), or 97\% of its upper bound of 0.2145, and no
wider-bound sensitivity analysis was conducted. We therefore cannot
establish whether the Emotion-dependent mapping would remain preferred
under a wider Early-LPP bound. The supported conclusion is narrower:
within the declared range and equal-complexity comparison, the
Emotion-dependent mapping predicted the recalled sets better.

A second near-bound estimate cautions against interpreting the
General-LPP coefficients in isolation. In the General-LPP fit with the
emotional-learning multiplier fixed, the source-learning scale
approached its upper bound: \(\hat\omega=9.047\) on the range
\((0,10]\). This pattern suggests that the source-learning scale and the
General Early-LPP coefficient may compensate for one another.

that the preferred model used both proposed mechanisms. The preferred
model, which combined preferential emotional learning with
Emotion-dependent LPP, estimated a source-learning scale of 0.930, an
emotional-learning multiplier of 1.946, and an emotion-dependent
log-learning slope of 0.128. The source-learning scale, context drift,
and choice sensitivity were estimated jointly with these terms, so
isolated parameter magnitudes should not be assigned direct
psychological meanings or compared across models without considering the
complete parameter vector.

The exploratory Full-LPP fits did not resolve whether a residual General

\subsubsection{Summary and scope}\label{summary-and-scope}

Taken together, the comparisons support complementary roles for
preferential emotional learning and Early-LPP modulation. The
emotional-learning multiplier reproduced the higher recall rate for
negative than neutral items. The item-level Early-LPP multiplier
distinguished which negative items were recalled. The preferred
specification combined preferential emotional learning with
Emotion-dependent LPP. It implements the proposed pathway from Early LPP
through source context-to-item learning strength to the conditional
probability of entering the recalled set, while also reproducing the
emotional-memory advantage. The comparison thereby implements the
original proposal to use an independent neural measure to constrain the
same source-learning pathway governed at the category level by
\(\phi_{\mathrm{emot}}\). The results show that LPP-derived scaling
complemented, rather than replaced, the fitted emotional-learning
multiplier.

The conclusion is deliberately narrower than a general account of
emotional memory. It concerns within-list variation in Early LPP and
recalled-item identity conditional on the observed recall count, using
one parameter vector shared across participants.

The analysis does not explain total recall or stopping and does not
estimate effects of between-list mean Early LPP. It also does not
characterize participant-specific mechanisms, compare source with
temporal implementations, or identify Early LPP uniquely with attention,
arousal, or stimulus significance. Those questions require different
data or additional models rather than stronger interpretation of the
present comparison.

Finally, the numerical conclusions are conditional on the 50-order
recalled-set approximation, the declared parameter bounds, and the
differential-evolution search. The mechanistic conclusions concern the
stated eCMR adaptation, including its componentwise context update and
its omission of semantic similarity and source-switch disruption. Within
those limits, a category-wide emotional-learning multiplier and an
emotion-dependent item-level neural multiplier accounted for
complementary structure in the observed recalled sets.
